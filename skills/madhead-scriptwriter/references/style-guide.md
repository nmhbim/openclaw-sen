# Style Guide

## Narrator Voice

Write as a first-person live narrator describing events as if they are unfolding in front of the viewer.
Keep the tone cold, technical, and immersive, like a deeply embedded war correspondent.

## Sentence Rhythm

- Use short, hard sentences when action escalates.
- Use longer, denser sentences for setup, systems, context, and technical explanation.
- Let rhythm tighten as the battlefield narrows.

## Emotional Method

Use show-don't-tell.
Do not state emotions directly.
Reveal fear, dread, shock, fatigue, or inevitability through concrete details.

### Strong examples
- Nobody had a return date.
- Nobody waves. They just stand and look.
- One soldier takes a selfie. Then puts the phone away.

## Technical Density

Use real weapons, systems, and specs when available.
Blend technical details naturally into the narration instead of listing them separately.

Examples of acceptable specificity:
- SM-6
- GBU-57 MOP
- AGM-88 HARM
- Phalanx CIWS at 4,500 rounds per minute
- Aegis Baseline 9
- AN/APS-145 radar

## Script Body Rules

- Write in continuous prose.
- Do not use bullet lists inside the main script body.
- Keep the narration visual, sequential, and scene-driven.
